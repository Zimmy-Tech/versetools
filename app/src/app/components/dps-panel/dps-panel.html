What is Real DPS?

This data is based on real in-game testing and results. It is my best understanding of how the system works, calculated to an error rate of less than 1.5%. It could possibly be incorrect, however, enough data points were checked that it seems incredibly likely. The rationale is listed here as well as in the formulas tab.

The fire rates listed in the game data for many weapons are not what you actually get in-game. We tested this by timing ammo consumption across 9 different weapons and found a consistent pattern.

The Problem

The game server runs on a clock that ticks 30 times per second. Every tick, the server checks: "is it time to fire another shot?" If yes, fire. If no, wait for the next tick.

A weapon that wants to fire every 80ms (750 RPM) gets delayed:

  • Tick 1 (0ms): FIRE
  • Tick 2 (33ms): not yet
  • Tick 3 (66ms): not yet
  • Tick 4 (100ms): 100ms > 80ms → FIRE

Instead of 80ms between shots, you get 100ms. That's 600 RPM instead of 750.

Which Weapons Are Affected?

Any weapon that fires from a cycling sequence of 2 or more shots is affected. This includes obvious repeaters and some weapons labeled "Cannon" in-game that are actually 2-entry sequences under the hood (like Lightstrike Cannons).

Not affected: Gatlings (continuous spin) and true single-shot cannons (1-entry sequences like M-Series). Their listed rates are accurate.

Rule of thumb: if the listed RPM doesn't divide evenly into 1800, the weapon is probably a sequence weapon being quantized down.

Why Some RPMs Land Cleanly

A weapon at 100 RPM, 225 RPM, 300 RPM, 450 RPM, 600 RPM, 900 RPM, or 1800 RPM lands exactly on a tick boundary — no loss. A weapon at 240 RPM, 350 RPM, 500 RPM, 750 RPM, or 825 RPM does not, and gets rounded down to the next clean value.

The Formula

Real RPM = 1800 / ceil(1800 / Listed RPM)

Example: Lightstrike Cannon listed at 240 RPM. 1800 / 240 = 7.5 → rounds up to 8 ticks per shot → 1800 / 8 = 225 RPM (a 6.3% loss).

How Shield Resistances Work ×

Every incoming shot is reduced by two independent mitigations from your shields:

  • Resist — reduces the damage your shield itself takes from the shot.
  • Absorb — reduces the damage that bleeds through to your hull from the shot.

Both apply to the same shot at the same time. The shield and the hull are each hit, just for different (smaller) amounts.

Worked Example

A weapon hits you for 100 physical damage. Your shield is up with 17% Resist and 30% Absorb:

  • Shield takes 100 × (1 − 0.17) = 83 damage
  • Hull takes 100 × (1 − 0.30) = 70 damage

So a 100‑damage shot deals 83 to the shield and 70 to the hull on the very same trigger pull. They are not a chain — they are two separate reductions.

Why Some Resists Are Negative

Ballistic shields favour stopping bullets (high physical resist) but are vulnerable to energy weapons. A negative energy resist means the shield takes more than 100% of the incoming energy damage — the shot is amplified against the shield itself.

Shielded Phys Deflect

Your armor's physical deflect threshold says "any shot with less than X alpha bounces off". With shields up, only the bleedthrough portion (governed by Absorb) ever reaches the armor in the first place, so a much bigger raw shot is required to push enough through to penetrate.

Shielded Phys Deflect = Armor Phys Deflect ÷ (1 − Phys Absorb)

Example: Armor deflect 80, Phys Absorb 30% → raw alpha needed = 80 ÷ 0.70 ≈ 114. Anything below that is fully blocked while shields are holding.
